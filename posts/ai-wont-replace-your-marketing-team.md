# AI Won't Replace Your Marketing Team. Bad Briefing Will.

Most founders are using AI tools wrong. They fire up ChatGPT, paste in a vague product description, and expect magic. What comes out sounds like every other company in their space — because that's exactly what it is.

The problem isn't the AI. The problem is the brief.

## The Input Problem

AI is a multiplier. If your input is vague, your output is vague at scale. If your input is sharp — if you know your customer's language, their specific pain, the exact thing that makes your product different — AI becomes genuinely powerful.

The founders who complain that AI content "sounds generic" are giving generic inputs. They're describing their product in the same terms their competitors use on their own websites.

## What a Good Brief Looks Like

A strong marketing brief answers three things:

1. **Who is this for, specifically?** Not "B2B SaaS founders." Try: "First-time founders at Series A who hired a marketing manager six months ago and are now questioning whether they hired the right person."
2. **What's the one thing we need them to believe?** Not "we're the best." Try: "Their current content process is creating the illusion of marketing activity without any revenue connection."
3. **What's the proof?** Specific case study, specific number, specific before/after.

With those three inputs, AI stops being a liability and starts being leverage.

## The Uncomfortable Truth

Most marketing teams aren't losing to AI. They're losing to founders who took the time to actually think through their positioning — and then used AI to execute it faster.

The bottleneck is never the tool. It's the clarity behind it.

If your content sounds like everyone else's, that's a strategy problem. Fix the strategy, and the content fixes itself.
